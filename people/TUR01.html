Entries for Turner as Thomas Turner begin in 1803, the year the young Thomas Turner sent his letter of introduction (‘I totter on the brink of perdition and call on you to save me…at 8o’clock this evening I shall call at your house’) dated 4 July 1803 – printed in St Clair pp. 300–01. The diary records his visit later that evening - and 'T Turner' listed in the '1796 list' under '1903' seems to further support this identification.

Turner married Cornelia Boinville in 1812 and later qualified as a lawyer (p. 301). See St Clair pp. 384–8 for a summary of the Turner/Shelley relationship/personal drama. But see also MS. Abinger c. 11, fols. 77-8: 28 Jan. 1812 Godwin to Patrickson, where Godwin says he presumes P has heard of TT’s marriage to Miss Boinville – TT says she will have £10,000 on her marriage – Godwin thinks he marries solely for money. Godwin refers to Turner in a later letter to Patrickson Dep. b.228.8-07 10.7.1812 in which he says that 'Turner is an unlucky specimen. And after all his letters were not worth writing, mere chips in porridge.'

There are a variety of other Turner entries distinguished by initials, but these are unidentified.

C Turner has not been coded as Mrs Cornelia Turner. In most cases these entries seem to have no grounds for linking the entry to Turner and his wife. For example, in 1814 C Turner calls from Mackintosh.

A further Mr Turner is a lawyer who appeared on the stage in the leading role of Macbeth in 1798, and whom Godwin saw on that occasion and subsequently in 1804. According to newspaper reports, the acting was not a success. This Turner seems unconnected to others.

T Turner is recorded on Godwin's '1796' list for 1803.
