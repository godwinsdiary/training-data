James Marshall was one of Godwin's oldest friends, whom he met first when at the Hoxton Academy. They remained close for the rest of their lives, although Marshall seems to have been the subordinate partner and is often described as Godwin's amenuensis. The frequency of their contact diminished after Godwin married, apparently because Mary Jane was not pleased to have Marshall constantly around the house. Marshall seems to have been involved in translating and editing, and he is thought to have transcibed a number of Godwin's letters and minor works. Marshall's independent spirit flourished only once, to our knowledge, when he read Caleb Williams and advised Godwin to consign it to the flames. There are a number of references to Marshall visiting Southampton and Guildford in September 1788 the significance of which is unclear . St Clair suggests that there was a plan for both to emigrate to the West Indies at one stage (p. 14), basing this on a letter from Mrs Marshall in 1784 (MS. Abinger c. 1, fols 16-17: 4 Sept. 1784). The letter clearly indicates a joint business investment (that went wrong) but it is not clear that emigration was their objective. It is not clear whether Mrs Marshall is James's wife or mother, but the tone of the letter suggests the former.

The two entries for Marshal on 30 July 1810 and 4 January 1811 seem likely not to be James Marshall, but sound like they are part of a law firm or similar - Charnley and Marshall.

There are problems in systematically distinguishing Marshall from Mary Shelley. because from Mary's birth, 30 August 1797, to her elopement with PBS, 28 July 1814, Godwin uses M to ref to both Marshall and Mary . She then disappears from the diary and returns at the end of 1816. From 27.5.1817 she is nearly always distinguished as MWS but in the few months from the end of 1816 to May 1817 she is denoted by several different variants: MWG, Mary and M.

The question of whether an entry is Marshall or Mary is difficult in the early years after Mary's birth. It is clear that Godwin sees Mary when she is a child much more often than he registers in the diary. TThe occasions on which she does appear in the diary when she is a child seem to be special outings but it is difficult to assess how frequent these are, and they are deduced largely by the presence of two M entries within a single event. Once Godwin is married to Mary Jane it seems likely that Mary and one or other of Mary Jane's own children might accompany her on occasion, suggesting that an entry that includes MJ, and one of F. C, or J (Fanny, Charles or Jane) and an M is more likely to be referring to Mary than top Marshall. The history of tension between Mary Jane and Marshall would reinforce such a judgment; on the other hand, the tension might be because Godwin included him so often!

We have assumed that Godwin and Mary Jane are not likely to include Mary in outings and dinners until she is at least three or four. But we cannot always be confident about this since some of the entries are ambiguous. For example, very soon after MW's death, on 23.9.1797 'E F dines: adv M, F, PV, Ht and Johnson' M has been coded as Marshall rather than Mary – but it could plausibly be Mary. However, we read the diary as suggesting that soon afterGodwin begins to restrict entries to Mary to special occasions.
