Godwin's elder brother John. J and John Godwin hav been included; Jno has been excluded as on a couple of occasions Godwin refers to both J and Jno appearing at the same time.

St Clair suggests (36) that in 1772 he is a clerk of the Inner Temple but that he took to drink and gambling and died in 1805.

Godwin records his death.

Godwin's younger brother Joseph. Godwin records his death in 1825. It seems likely that he dies in prison.

Joseph Godwin seems to have been apprenticed to Thomas Raven of Fakenham, grocer and draper, in 1771 (National Archive IR1/58 f24). Raven may have been the father in law of Philip Hull Godwin. Jospeh married Mary Jex on 1 Devember 1776, and had six children, John Jex (1780), Maryy Anne (1781), Charles (1783), Harriet 1785), Phebe 1788, and William 1792. Joseph seems to have moved to London and become the agent of Francis and Thomas Lea of Kiderminster, at their London warehouse in 10, Bagnio Court, Newgate Street. (JG refers to lea in his letter from Bagnio Court in July 1801). Joseph died in the Fleet Prison in 1825 after imprisonment for debt in 1821. Godwin notes his death. He also notes the death of Mrs Jo Godwin: 'Mrs Jo Godwin dies', 1834-03-05.

  • Name: Godwin, Hannah
  • Gender: Female
  • Birth Date:
  • Death Date: 27  December  1817
  • Occupation: dressmaker

Hannah Godwin was William's only sister. She came to London to find work and was employed as a dress-maker - as St Clair notes (p. 37), 'one of the most exploited trades open to a respectable young woman.' However, she seems to have succeeeded to a certain degree. She was in partnership with Frances and Anna French (sisters of Charlotte Fawcett, Joseph Fawcett's wife). She employed a number of young women as apprentices (several of whom appear in the diary (Ann Potts, Elizabeth Axtel(l), Ann Hansard, and Charlotte Littlewood. Other apprentices do not appear in the diary.

There is evidence of trouble between Hannah Godwin and James Marshall. In 1797, Godwin's mother comments that her daughter's character is being harmed by Marshall dinning alone on Sundays with her. And In 1800, there is a letter from Hannah to Godwin welcoming him back, but refusing to come and see him if Marshall is going to be there because of some episode (MS. Abinger c.3, fols. 64-5 and c. 6, fol. 113)(Later entries suggest that their disagreement is resolved). Hannah and Godwin saw each other a good deal when she was first in London. Their relationship seems to have cooled following what seems to have been Hannah's encouragement of Fell's marriage to one of her apprentices and Dibbin's marriage to Louisa Jones.

It has been assumed that Godwin is consistent in the notation of family names. Therefore, all H Godwin's have been assumed to be Hannah while Ht Godwin has assumed to be his niece, Harriet in all cases. Hannah is also referred to as Miss Godwin in the early years of the diary (comparing social circles in 1797 and 1798 when he changes notation gives a high degree of correlation).

There does seem to be one case in which H G is used for Hull Godwin - when he refers to H G and family (5) on 4 September 1805, when he is visiting Norfolk. But otherwise the references do seem consistently to be to Hannah Godwin.

  • Name: Godwin, Nathaniel
  • Gender: Male
  • Birth Date: 19  February  1768
  • Occupation: journeyman

Nathaniel, Godwin's youngest brother, went to sea. St Clair suggests he was pressed in 1788, and that there is evidence of him having left the Navy to start a business, then failing and resorting to the positon of a journeyman servant. Family correspondence suggests that he was, for a number of years in the service of Mr Hurry (his deceased brother Conyers had been in the service of Captain Hurry - see the entry to Ives Hurry). In 1799 Godwin's mother writes to him that 'Natty is entered on ye 5th year at Munton's' Abinger MS c. 4, fols. 96-7, and there are later references to Munton as a shopkeeeper. Nathaniel seems to have had an attempt at starting his own business, only to fail, and he also joined with Joseph in his carpet warehous. He is entered as a pensioner at Charterhouse in 1827, aged 60.

  • Name: Godwin, Harriet
  • Gender: Female
  • Death Date: 5  March  1834

Harriet is Godwin's niece, the daughter of Joseph Godwin. St Clair suggests that she is married to Godwin's brother, Joseph (see St Clair , p. 247 - in contrast to P. Marshall's claim that Joseph was married to Mary (p. 13)). The evidence suggests that Joseph is married to Mary Jex.

There is an intense period of anxiety with respect to Harriet in 1804, for which there is evidence in the National Archive. Godwin helps his brother Joseph between 23 February and 27 February 1804 when Harriet runs off with Thomas West. Thomas West had earlier expressed interest in marrying Harriet, but in June 1803 he had married Ms Lucy Dallas, the sister of Sir George Dallas. On 17 January 1804 Harriet ran off with West to Lewes, where West held a post in the army. Godwin and Joseph (Harriet's father) went to Lewes but did not see West until they arranged for Lord Craven, the colonel of West's regiment, to summon him. They were still denied access to Harriet. The affadavit to Judge Grose was to bring Harriet before him. Harriet, however, remained with West. On 13 April Godwin and Mary Jane visited Mrs West and Lady Dallas. West on behalf of Jo Godwin. He does so very shortly before a trip to the south coast, where he then calls on West in or near Lewes, and then exchanges letters and calls over the next few days. The later reference to Ht West at Jo Godwin's is in fact references to Harriet Godwin and has been coded as such. Mrs West in 1804 is West's wife and has been coded separately.

It has been assumed that Godwin is consistent in the notation of family names. Therefore, all H Godwin's have been assumed to be Hannah while Ht Godwin has assumed to be Harriet in all cases.
